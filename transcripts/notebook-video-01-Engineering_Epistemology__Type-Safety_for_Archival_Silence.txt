When we query a standard academic database for a literature review, the results we get back are dictated by the database's own indexing rules. We map out citations, aggregate the literature, and build our foundation on data that is inherently biased toward what those servers store. These standard systems index what survived and what was officially published. They systematically exclude oral traditions, sensor documents, and knowledge produced by colonized or illiterate populations. By relying exclusively on institutional infrastructure, the resulting research map silently writes ordinary people entirely out of the historical record. In 1966, historian E. P. Thompson identified this exact problem. He coined the approach history from below, arguing that conventional historiography relies almost entirely on sources produced and about the elite. In modern research, Thompson's critique of elite archival bias is usually relegated to a vague methodology paragraph, rather than treated as a structural flaw in the evidence itself. This verbal apology stands in sharp contrast to the extreme rigidity of our actual data models. Standard academic schemas, like Bibtech or DOI-backed JSON objects, physically reject any input that lacks formal text-based institutional metadata. To address epistemic bias, we have to stop treating it as a theoretical footnote. We need to model what is missing as a measurable property of the data architecture itself. The solution is a dual schema architecture built using Zod V4 and TypeScript. Zod is a schema declaration and validation library that ensures data strictly matches a defined structure before a program accepts it. The first file, research source schema, defines what you possess, cataloging the sources and their metadata. The second file, research map assessment, executes a rigorous audit of what you do not have and categorizes exactly why it is missing. These two schemas operate on completely different timelines. The assessment is performed iteratively on the map as a whole, never embedded statically inside individual source records. To accommodate all types of evidence, the source schema enforces a core architectural rule. It is sparse by default. It requires only five core fields for any entry, ID, Kind, Title, Attribution and Providence. Because everything else is optional, a fully annotated museum archival record and a sparse URL stub for an oral history share the exact same type definition. Separating the map from the assessment allows us to build a container for diverse media from journal articles to Europa praise poems, without forcing them into a singular Western academic template. Consider how traditional databases handle temporal data. They demand exact isoformatted dates. Forcing a precise date like January 1, 1940 onto an oral tradition or an ancient artifact is intellectually dishonest. It requires the researcher to lie to the database just to bypass validation errors. To solve this, the schema introduces the fuzzy date sawed union, allowing one variable to accept distinctly different formats. If an exact it accepts an approximate date object, capturing a label, absolute bounds, and a mathematical confidence score. Standard schemas also enforce strict rules around authorship, demanding named individual authors. This schema replaces that requirement with an attribution object that includes a communal attribution parameter. A researcher can now accurately attribute a Europa proverb or a folk song to an ethnic group or a specific collective workshop without inventing an individual author to satisfy a required field. Data types must be designed to reflect the physical reality of the source medium, rather than distorting the source to satisfy the arbitrary constraints of the database. We also run into mechanical problems when trying to cite specific evidence within non-textual sources. Standard citation managers rely exclusively on page numbers. This addressing system fails for visual art, film, or music. The schema solves this with a structured locator. This is a discriminated union that splits into five distinct addressing modes, page, time, spatial, section, and free. The spatial locator utilizes an X and Y bounding box. This allows a researcher to cite a specific quadrant of a painting directly. The time locator accepts exact start and end seconds, isolating a specific claim within an hour-long audio field recording. The section locator targets structured works, allowing you to cite a precise movement or bar in a musical composition. Implementing polymorphic locators is the only way to treat oral, visual, and written evidence with equal epistemic rigor. We can point to a brushstroke exactly the way we point to a paragraph. Once the source map is populated, we shift to the second schema, the map assessment, to audit the results. The Herffindall Hirschman Index measures concentration by squaring and summing category shares. A map dominated 87% by journal articles returns an HHI approaching 1.0. This systematically excludes the oral, visual, and archival sources crushed into the remaining fraction. The assessment requires researchers to evaluate their map along specific power axes, such as class, race, and colonial position, declaring exactly who is over or underrepresented. It also enforces anthropologist Michel Ralph Trulio's distinction regarding archival silences. You must specify whether an absence exists in this particular map, or if it is an absence in the historical record itself. There is a massive difference between a source absence, like missing Moroccan soldiers' testimony that could be found with additional effort, and an archival destruction, where records were intentionally burned during a conflict and no longer exist. Forcing a researcher to encode exactly why a voice is missing using a type-safe enum prevents them from hiding behind the lazy excuse that no data is available. Noting a gap in your research without proposing a method to fix it is performative epistemology. The assessment schema models a blind spot as a first-class data entity. It is assigned its own UUID, a universally unique identifier, and a calculated severity score. This blind spot is then linked directly via a data array to the specific research claims at Weekens. We then deploy the remediation action array. This connects the named blind spots to concrete, assignable tasks designed to fill the gap. Each remediation action requires specific inputs, estimated researcher days to complete, expected impact on the map's balance, and a distinct action type, such as fieldwork, translation, or direct community engagement. True research rigor requires tracking the physical labor needed to fix your epistemic blind spots exactly the way an engineer tracks and patches a software bike. At the end of the audit, all components are aggregated into a final data object, the assessment verdict. The verdict includes a claim-supported field with four strict levels of validation. The lowest level is categorized as unsupported for stated scope. This operates as the Thomson test. If you claim to study the working-class experience, but your evidence consists entirely of elite parliamentary debates, your research fails at the schema level. The schema does document its own limitations. It relies entirely on the researcher's self-reported judgment and defaults to Western academic categorizations of power. Type safety cannot resurrect destroyed archives. It cannot automatically compute a perfectly balanced view of history. We know we will never entirely eliminate bias, but enforcing epistemic self-assessment as executable code ensures that structural silences are explicitly named, measured, and tracked. Never silently absorbed into an illusion of completeness.

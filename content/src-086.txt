Abstract
Background

Few studies have focused on the descriptive epidemiology and trends of HIV/AIDS since the publication of the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) study in 2021. Therefore, this study aims to analyze the trends and patterns of the HIV/AIDS burden at the global, regional, and national levels by sex, age and social development index (SDI). We also explored risk factors and predicted the GBD of HIV/AIDS until 2030.

Methods

Data on the etiology of HIV/AIDS from 1990 to 2021 were collected from the 2021 GBD database. Estimated annual percent changes (EAPCs) were calculated to assess temporal trends in the age-standardized incidence rate (ASIR), age-standardized disability-adjusted life-year rate (ASDR) and age-standardized mortality rate (ASMR) of HIV/AIDS patients. Measures categorized by sex, region, age and SDI quintiles. In addition, an age‒period‒cohort model was established to forecast future trends of the HIV/AIDS burden to 2030.

Results

Globally, the incidence of HIV/AIDS reached 1.65 million (95% uncertainty interval 1.48 to 1.82) in 2021. From 1990 to 2021, the global ASIR of HIV/AIDS showed a downward trend, with an EAPC of 0.14% (0.12–0.16%). The ASIR, ASDR, and ASMR of HIV/AIDS were highest in the low-SDI region and lowest in the high-SDI region. Regionally, southern sub-Saharan Africa had the highest ASIR in 2021, whereas Oceania had the largest increase in the ASIR from 1990 to 2021. Burundi (EAPC 14.04%, 95% CI -13.63 to -14.44%), followed by Burkina Faso, exhibited the most significant decrease in the ASIR. Globally, the highest incidence rate was observed in the 25–29 years old group. Unsafe sex is the primary factor leading to the burden of HIV/AIDS. By 2030, the global ASIR, ASDR and ASMR of HIV/AIDS are expected to gradually decrease.

Conclusion

HIV/AIDS disease burden varies by region, sex, and age. The global burden of HIV/AIDS is substantial in low-income regions, especially in Sub-Saharan Africa. Our study offers valuable epidemiological evidence for future research on the prevention and treatment of HIV/AIDS.

Background

Acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) is an infectious disease caused by human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). Since its discovery in 1980 [1], HIV has rapidly spread globally, posing a significant threat to public health. With the widespread use of highly active antiretroviral therapy, the patterns of HIV/AIDS prevalence and disease burden have changed rapidly, extending the life expectancy of those with HIV/AIDS and transforming it from a fatal disease to a chronic infectious disease [2]. Understanding trends in the burden of HIV/AIDS is crucial for assessing progress toward eliminating the epidemic and formulating policies aimed at preventing and managing the burden of HIV/AIDS.

According to data from the World Health Organization (WHO) and the Joint United Nations Programme on HIV/AIDS (UNAIDS), by 2023, approximately 39.9 million people globally were living with HIV, and approximately 630,000 people had died from AIDS-related disease [3]. Disease burden can also be expressed in disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), an indicator that measures the difference between perfect health and living with disease. DALYs are calculated by adding the years of life lost due to disability to the years of life lost due to death. The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Health Metrics and Evaluation Institute measures, compares, and reports health losses due to diseases using DALYs [4]. The 2019 GBD study comprehensively described the global burden of HIV/AIDS [5–11]. However, the burden of HIV/AIDS varies over time between countries and regions.

GBD 2021 is a global database that integrates analytical tools and represents the important outcomes of long-term cooperation among the world’s communities [12]. This resource allows for a deep understanding of the burden of HIV/AIDS. Here, we analyzed the changes in HIV/AIDS incidence, mortality, and disease burden from 1990 to 2021 across different regions, genders, and age groups based on the GBD 2021 database, aiming to provide a theoretical basis for the precise prevention and control of HIV/AIDS worldwide.

Methods
Data sources

The GBD 2021 study provides epidemiological estimates for 371 diseases and injuries across different sexes and ages in 204 countries, regions, and 21 regions from 1990–2021 [12]. GBD 2021 utilizes Disease Modeling Meta-Regression (DisMod-MR) 2.1, a Bayesian meta-regression tool, to estimate the prevalence and incidence of HIV/AIDS.

The annual data on HIV/AIDS cases by country were downloaded from the GBD 2021 database via the Global Health Data Exchange (GHDx) query tool (http://ghdx.healthdata.org/gbd-results-tool). The parameters considered in the query included time period, sex, age group, geographic location, metric, and measure. The incidence number, incidence rate, and disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) number, DALY rate, death number, and mortality rate were extracted from 1990 to 2020 for AIDS/HIV. Risk factor data associated with HIV/AIDS were also obtained.

In the GBD 2021 study, “unsafe sex” is defined as sexual behavior that increases the risk of disease transmission through sexual contact. This includes all instances of cervical cancer and sexually transmitted infections (STIs), except for those acquired congenitally. The selection of the three risk factors—unsafe sex, intimate partner violence, and injection drug use—was based on data availability in the GBD database and their established epidemiological relevance to HIV/AIDS burden, as identified in prior literature and global health guidelines. Additionally, a portion of HIV cases is categorized under unsafe sex, with this fraction being determined based on data that reports the proportion of HIV incidence attributable to sexual transmission. All the data are presented numerically along with the UIs.

We performed an age-period-cohort (APC) analysis via the BAPC package to predict the numbers and age-standardized rates of incidence, prevalence, and DALYs due to HIV/AIDS from 2020 to 2030.

The BAPC package utilizes a Poisson distribution assumption. We organized the burden of HIV/AIDS and projected population data into continuous five-year age intervals ranging from under 5 years old to 85 years and above. The population data for the years 1990–2030 were obtained from GBD 2021. Additionally, the GBD global population age standard was referenced against the GBD 2021 study.

Results
Trends in HIV/AIDS incidence from 1990 to 2021

The age-standardized incidence rate (ASIR) of HIV/AIDS varies significantly worldwide. In 2021, Lesotho had the highest ASIR (818.4 per 100,000 people), followed by Mozambique and Equatorial Guinea (Fig. 1A, Table S1). In terms of absolute numbers, South Africa had the highest number of cases in 2021 (233,500 (95% uncertainty interval (UI) 168,200 to 305,900)), followed by Mozambique and Nigeria (Table S2).

The most significant ASIR reduction was in 0 (EAPC = -14.04%, 95% CI -13.63 to -14.44%), followed by Burkina Faso and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Pakistan experienced the most significant increase in ASIR (EAPC = 31.16%, 95% CI 26.90–35.57%) (Fig. 1C).

HIV/AIDS-related burden from 1990 to 2021 across SDI quintiles

In all SDI quintiles, low-SDI regions presented the highest number of ASIRs from 1990 to 2021 (Fig. 2A). However, since 1993, the ASIR in low-SDI regions has shown a decreasing trend (EAPC: -4.83 (95% CI -5.09 to -4.58)) (Figure S1, Table S4). Among the 21 geographic regions, southern sub-Saharan Africa had the highest ASIR in 2021 (334.3 per 100,000 people (95% UI 259.7 to 422.3)), whereas eastern sub-Saharan Africa showed the most significant decline (EAPC: -5.35% 95% CI -5.68% to -5.01%) (Table S4).

Globally, the age-standardized DALY rate (ASDR) associated with HIV/AIDS has increased by 45.43% (42.89–48.62%) over the past 30 years. From 1990 to 2021, high-SDI regions experienced the greatest decrease in ASDRs (EAPC = -6.02% (95% CI -6.74 to -5.31%)) (Figure S1). Regions with higher SDI levels generally maintained lower ASDRs, whereas low-SDI regions peaked in ASDRs in 2001 and have since shown a continued decline (Fig. 2B).

Among the 21 geographic regions, low-income southern sub-Saharan Africa had the highest ASDR in 2021 (9,615.8 per 100,000 (95% CI 9,003.0 to 10,270.0)), and Australasia presented the most significant declining trend in ASIRs (EAPC: -7.86% (95% CI -8.77 to -6.95%)) (Figure S1).

The trend in ASDRs mirrors the trend in mortality rates. In the SDI quintiles, low-SDI regions had the highest age-standardized mortality rates (ASMRs) from 1990 to 2021, followed by lower-middle-SDI regions. From 1997 to 2021, high-SDI regions presented the lowest number of ASMRs (Fig. 2C). ASMRs in low-SDI regions peaked in 2001 and have since shown a continued decline.

Among the 21 geographic regions, low-income southern sub-Saharan Africa had the highest ASMR in 2021 (168.1 per 100,000 (95% UI 158.9 to 178.6)), and Australasia presented the most significant decline in ASIRs (EAPC: -7.86% (95% CI -8.77 to -6.95%)) (Figure S1). Eastern sub-Saharan Africa, western sub-Saharan Africa, and southern sub-Saharan Africa had the highest number of deaths (Table S5).

Correlation between SDI and HIV/AIDS burden

The ASIR of HIV/AIDS shows a moderate negative correlation with the SDI (cor = -0.262, p = 1.684e-12). Similarly, age-standardized DALYs and mortality rates also exhibited comparable relationships (Fig. 3A-C).

In sub-Saharan Africa, the HIV/AIDS burden initially increased with increasing SDI but then began to decline. In contrast, the other 17 regions generally maintained a stable burden. Southern sub-Saharan Africa is the only region where the ASIR, DALY, and mortality rates have consistently been higher than expected on the basis of the SDI over the past 30 years.

HIV/AIDS burden by gender

Similar to the overall population trends, the ASIR for both females and males increased from 1990 to 1996, followed by a sustained decline. From 1990 to 2004, the ASIR for females was greater than that for males, but the difference between the two groups gradually decreased thereafter (Fig. 4A).

HIV/AIDS burden by age group in 2021

The HIV/AIDS incidence rate peaks in females aged 25–29 years, reaching 59.09 per 100,000 (95% UI 51.62 to 68.16) in 2021. For females aged 5–29 years, the incidence rate increases with age, whereas for females over 25 years, the incidence rate decreases with age. In males, the incidence rate peaks in the 30–34 years age group, reaching 59.75 per 100,000 (95% UI 52.65 to 67.49) (Fig. 5A). The incidence rate for females was higher than that for males in the 15–29 years age range, whereas males had a higher incidence rate than females after the age of 30 years (Fig. 5A).

The DALY rate for HIV/AIDS in females peaks in the 30–34 age group, reaching 1111.38 per 100,000 (95% UI 881.76 to 1414.32) in 2021. For females aged 5–39 years, the DALY rate increases with age, whereas it decreases for those over 40 years. The mortality rate for females was higher than that for males in the 15–34 years age range, whereas males had a higher mortality rate than females after the age of 35 years (Fig. 5C).

Risk factors for HIV/AIDS burden

This study evaluated the contributions of three risk factors to HIV/AIDS-related DALYs in 1990 and 2021. Among these risk factors, unsafe sex was the primary contributor, followed by drug use (Fig. 6A-B). Both unsafe sex and drug use have increasingly contributed to HIV/AIDS DALY rates across all age groups.

Global disease burden prediction for HIV/AIDS

On the basis of comprehensive GBD data from 1990 to 2021, we utilized the BAPC model to project the age-standardized incidence. By 2030, the global ASIR, ASDR and ASMR of HIV/AIDS are expected to gradually decrease (Fig. 7A–F). The shaded areas in the figure indicate that the disease burden of HIV/AIDS could fluctuate dramatically as the corresponding rates rise or fall by 1% per year.

Discussion

To date, our study is the first to present the global burden of HIV/AIDS using GBD 2021 data, which are widely regarded as reliable and standardized, though variations in data methodology may exist across countries. We conducted a detailed temporal and regional analysis of HIV incidence, DALYs, and mortality across different countries and SDI levels, providing a more granular understanding of the epidemic. Furthermore, our study also includes long-term projections up to 2030, utilizing Bayesian modeling to forecast future trends under various epidemic control scenarios. By integrating these findings with broader epidemiological trends and policy implications, we provide a more comprehensive and forward-looking view of the global HIV/AIDS burden, enhancing the value of our work beyond the publicly available GBD data.

Our results show that the burden of HIV/AIDS varies significantly worldwide, with the highest burden concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa. The burden of HIV/AIDS is greater among young and middle-aged people, especially in low-income regions. Unsafe sexual behavior is the primary factor contributing to the burden of HIV/AIDS. Although the burden of HIV/AIDS is expected to decrease further by 2030, there is still a long way to go to eliminate AIDS, and efforts to strengthen HIV/AIDS prevention and treatment in high-burden areas are essential.

The HIV/AIDS disease burden reported in our study shows slight variations compared to previous research. According to UNAIDS, there were an estimated 1.5 million new infections and 650,000 AIDS-related deaths in 2021 [13]. GBD 2021 employed distinct modeling approaches compared to UNAIDS. Specifically, the EPP-ASM (Estimation and Projection Package Age-Sex Model) and Spectrum models used in GBD estimation differ in several aspects, including the structure of the models, model parameters, and the specific data sources used for calibration. While both models rely on UNAIDS Spectrum estimates, GBD incorporates additional data sources, such as vital registration data, household surveys, and sentinel surveillance, which may contribute to variations in the estimates [14]. Moreover, the underlying assumptions regarding HIV transmission dynamics, demographic factors, and treatment status may also lead to differences in our results compared to UNAIDS.

The burden of HIV/AIDS varies significantly worldwide, with the highest burden concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa. Two-thirds of global HIV infections are concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa [15]. Economic factors are important to the high prevalence of HIV/AIDS in sub-Saharan Africa. Our study revealed that from 1990 to 2021, the incidence, mortality, and DALY rates of HIV/AIDS in low-SDI (sociodemographic index) regions were higher than those in the other four regions, whereas the indicators in high-SDI regions were almost all lower than those in other regions. These findings indicate that the disease burden of HIV/AIDS is closely related to the sociodemographic index of a region. High-SDI regions have better social infrastructure, greater social welfare, and relatively easier access to medical services and medications [16]. People in high-SDI regions also have a more comprehensive understanding of disease transmission routes and preventive measures, with more individuals having self-protection awareness. In contrast, people in low-SDI regions often lack awareness and have limited resources for prevention and intervention, making them more vulnerable to HIV/AIDS infection. Therefore, reducing the HIV/AIDS burden clearly requires improving a region’s economic development alongside corresponding health education and response measures [17]. Programs such as South Africa’s IMAGE initiative demonstrate that economic empowerment, when paired with health education, not only mitigates material deprivation but also disrupts risk pathways like transactional sex and gender-based violence. Similarly, Thailand’s 100% Condom Program and Brazil’s universal ART access highlight how structural reforms—from decriminalization to healthcare democratization—can normalize prevention and treatment uptake. Policymakers should prioritize the development of multisectoral frameworks that combine economic support systems, such as vocational training and microgrants, with scalable health innovations like mobile PrEP clinics and telemedicine. Additionally, strengthening grassroots advocacy to challenge discriminatory norms is essential. Addressing the underlying drivers of HIV vulnerability—such as poverty, educational disparities, and marginalization—is critical. Without tackling these root causes, even the most advanced biomedical strategies may fail to achieve equitable outcomes in HIV prevention and care.

Notably, Burundi, followed by Burkina Faso, exhibited the most significant decrease in the ASIR. This remarkable progress is largely attributable to the country’s sustained political commitment and systematic implementation of national strategies. Since 2002, Burundi has developed and executed five consecutive national HIV/AIDS strategic plans (2002–2006, 2007–2011, 2012–2016, 2014–2017, and 2018–2022), aiming to expand prevention, testing, and treatment services in a comprehensive manner [18]. Furthermore, Burundi became the second country in West and Central Africa to achieve the UNAIDS “95-95-95” targets—indicating that 95% of people living with HIV know their status, 95% of those diagnosed receive treatment, and 95% of those on treatment achieve viral suppression [19]. These milestones establish Burundi as a model for effective HIV/AIDS control among developing countries.

In our study, we observed that the HIV burden in sub-Saharan Africa exhibits distinct patterns compared to other regions, particularly with the high prevalence of heterosexually-driven epidemics. Globally, women and girls accounted for 44% of new HIV infections in 2023, but in sub-Saharan Africa, this proportion rose to 62%, with adolescent girls and young women aged 15–24 being disproportionately affected [13]. Approximately 3,100 new infections occur weekly in this group, nearly all within sub-Saharan Africa. These trends highlight the significant impact of gender inequality, socio-cultural factors, and differential access to healthcare services, which are not captured by the Social Development Index (SDI). While SDI helps to explain broader patterns, the unique epidemiology of HIV in sub-Saharan Africa is influenced by factors such as gender-based vulnerabilities, sexual behavior, and public health policies. Therefore, we acknowledge that SDI alone is insufficient to fully account for the HIV epidemic in this region and that more targeted research is needed to address the complex social, cultural, and healthcare-related drivers of HIV transmission in sub-Saharan Africa.

The global burden of HIV/AIDS is greater among women than men. This is partly due to women having an additional mode of transmission—intimate partner violence—which increases their vulnerability. Additionally, women are more susceptible to HIV/AIDS during sexual intercourse, particularly in the context of unsafe sexual practices [20, 21]. Women are more vulnerable to HIV infection because they have a larger and more susceptible genital mucosa, making HIV more easily transmitted through sexual contact [22]. In addition to biological vulnerabilities, region-specific, age-specific, and society-specific factors significantly contribute to the higher HIV burden among women and girls, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. Gender inequalities, limited access to education and healthcare, and socio-cultural practices such as early marriage, early sexual debut, and gender-based violence exacerbate their risk [23].

Our study revealed that the peak burden of HIV/AIDS occurs among people aged 25–44 years, indicating that young and middle-aged individuals constitute a high-risk group for AIDS. This age group is characterized by youth, vitality, and a preference for excitement and novelty [24], making them more susceptible to external stimuli and risky behaviors such as promiscuity [25], drug use [26], and needle sharing [20]. Analysis of the risk factors for the burden of HIV/AIDS indicates that unsafe sexual behavior is the primary risk factor. Our findings underscore that unsafe sexual behavior is a key driver of HIV/AIDS transmission among young (15–24 years), a pattern supported by global evidence highlighting the effectiveness of targeted prevention education. In youth, heightened neuroplasticity enhances the impact of interventions addressing risk perception and decision-making [27], with school-based programs reducing unprotected sex by 40% [28]. Education on HIV/AIDS for young people has proven to be an effective preventive strategy [29].

We applied the Bayesian model of APC to predict the burden of HIV/AIDS from 2022 to 2030, considering age, period, and cohort factors. Our results showed that the burden of HIV/AIDS will decrease annually in the future, but the global goal of ending HIV/AIDS by 2030 is off track. The global community will need to defy expectations. The end of the AIDS epidemic will require substantial investment in funds and personnel from governments worldwide.

This study has several limitations. First, the results are based on model calculations and may differ from actual values. Second, The GBD 2021 data used in this study includes information only up to 2021 and does not incorporate more recent data, such as the UNAIDS 2024 HIV report. This limitation may affect the accuracy of our findings, as newer data could potentially reveal changes in HIV/AIDS burden and trends that were not captured in the 2021 dataset. Third, there are numerous variables, such as the upcoming availability of long-acting injectable treatments like lenacapavir for PrEP, ongoing developments in HIV treatment, and potential changes in PEPFAR funding, which introduce additional uncertainty and could impact the accuracy of future projections.

Conclusions

In conclusion, HIV/AIDS is a significant global health issue, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. While the overall burden of HIV/AIDS is declining, it is increasing in a few countries. Additionally, HIV/AIDS is highly prevalent among young and middle-aged people in low-income regions. Therefore, to eliminate the epidemic of HIV/AIDS, strengthening HIV prevention in high-burden countries and regions and focusing more on young and middle-aged populations are crucial.

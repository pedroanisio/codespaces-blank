The Americans with Disabilities Act is signed, July 26, 1990
By ANDREW GLASS
07/25/2015 10:59 PM EDT
Updated: 07/26/2015 08:09 AM EDT

On this day in 1990, President George H.W. Bush signed the Americans with Disabilities Act into law. The bipartisan legislation required most employers to provide “reasonable accommodations” to employees with disabilities while making nearly all public accommodations accessible to disabled persons. The law was subsequently amended, with the changes going into effect on Jan. 1, 2009.

The wide-ranging civil rights law bars discrimination based on disability. It affords similar protections against discrimination to Americans with disabilities as the Civil Rights Act of 1964, which outlawed discrimination based on race, religion, sex, national origin and other characteristics. The covered disabilities include both mental and physical medical conditions.

Rep. Romano Mazzoli (D-Ky.) declared after Bush signed the bill: “Mr. speaker, today is a day to rejoice both for the millions of disabled Americans who, because of ADA, will enter a new era and for the Congress of the United States and the president whose collaborative efforts led to the passage of this milestone law.”

Sen. Tom Harkin (D-Iowa) served as the bill’s chief sponsor in the Senate. In introducing the legislation, Harkin delivered part of his speech in sign language because, he said, he wanted his deaf brother to be able to understand its provisions.

On May 9, 1989, Rep. Tony Coelho (D-Calif.) introduced the House version, which the lawmakers approved later that month. Harkin introduced his version on the same date. It cleared the upper chamber, also with large majorities, later that summer. More than a year later, the Senate version arrived in the House for a final vote. It was approved 377-28 with 27 not voting.

In the months leading up to final passage, some 100 demonstrators were arrested in the Rotunda of the U.S. Capitol after meeting with top lawmakers to demand the bill’s quick passage.

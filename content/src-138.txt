Development Alternatives with Women for a New Era (DAWN) has been a key force in feminist scholarship, activism, and advocacy from the Global South since its inception in 1984. As DAWN marks its 40th anniversary, we invite you to explore a timeline that traces the dynamic evolution of feminist movements over the past four decades. The left side maps key moments, changes, campaigns, and mobilisations within regional and global feminist struggles—highlighting how changes in the Global South have affected women’s rights and how women across these regions have advanced transformative agendas for justice and equality. The right side traces DAWN’s institutional journey, showing how the organisation emerged, adapted, and responded to shifting political, social, and economic landscapes and contributed to the wider feminist movements. Together, these intertwined threads offer a nuanced perspective on DAWN’s role in shaping—and being shaped by—collective efforts toward social change and justice for women in the Global South.

In the Global South, anti-colonial movements gained momentum, with struggles against colonial powers and dictatorships across Africa and Latin America and resistance to nuclear testing in the Pacific, among others. Feminist movements expanded globally, linking gender justice with critiques of capitalism, militarism, and imperialism.

A DECADE FOR WOMEN

The UN Decade for Women, as it labeled in the first UN World Conference on Women in Mexico City, saw the rise of powerful feminist, anti-authoritarian, anti-nuclear movements across the Global South that challenged colonial legacies, the economic violence of neoliberal economic policies, and patriarchal norms—laying the groundwork for transnational feminist solidarity and critiques of global inequality.

1984

1984
Bangalore,
India
DAWN’S FIRST MEETING

DAWN’s first sparks emerged from a meeting in Bangalore, India, in August 1984. The encounter was called in preparation for the Third World Conference on Women, scheduled to take place in Nairobi the following year to asses the progress of the UN Decade for Women (1975-1985).

Women from the global South convened to share their regional and national experiences, discussing the rise of religious fundamentalism, now a common topic, as well as militarism and the testing of nuclear weapons. They also explored the debt crisis of the 1980s, among other geopolitical themes and their connections to women’s rights.

Building on this encounter, DAWN initiated its first analysis, linking these topics and situating them within the context of the global South, colonialism, and the international development agenda.

1985

1985
RISING AGAINST STRUCTURAL ADJUSTMENT PROGRAMS (SAPS)

In the 1980s, Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) imposed by the IMF and World Bank prioritised debt repayment over social welfare, dismantling public services and worsening poverty, unemployment, and food insecurity in the Global South. Widespread protests and critical UN interventions exposed the human toll of neoliberal austerity and economic restructuring.

1985
Bergen, Norway
Who needs a larger share of a poisoned pie? Drafting DAWN first analysis

Numerous conferences and seminars were held on all five continents following the Bangalore meeting to gather as many diverse views and experiences as possible, which were then debated and incorporated into a draft that would become DAWN’s first book. A few months later, with support from the Norwegian government and the Ford Foundation, a group came together to look at the final draft, which was written in just three weeks. Gita Sen, who would become one of DAWN’s co-founders, recalls the energy and urgency of that moment, which propelled the group to the 1985 Nairobi UN Conference and laid the foundation for DAWN’s intellectual legacy.




Their critical feminist approach was encapsulated in this single powerful question: a challenge to mainstream development thinking.

1985
Nairobi, Kenya
DAWN’S FIRST BOOK, CHANGING THE NARRATIVE ON DEVELOPMENT

Published as a book written by Gita Sen and Caren Grown, “Development, Crises and Alternative Visions” made a significant impact at the UN Conference on Women in Nairobi in 1985 and was considered a game-changer in feminist literature for bringing the voices of women from the economic South to the forefront of the global women’s movement.

DAWN’s panels and workshops at the forum focused on the effects of growth-oriented development, the economic, political and cultural crises, and alternative visions and methods for the women’s movement. By the end of the conference, over 2,000 women had participated in panels and workshops, endorsing DAWN’s vision and goals.

The Institute of Social Studies Trust (ISST) in New Delhi, India, housed DAWN Secretariat during that year, up until it was relocated to the Instituto Universitário de Pesquisas do Rio de Janeiro (IUPERJ), with Neuma Aguiar as general coordinator.

1990

1990
REDEFINING JUSTICE

In the aftermath of the Cold War, the disintegration of Yugoslavia and the Rwandan genocide marked a turning point in international justice, as wartime sexual violence was formally recognised as a crime against humanity. These events catalysed the emergence of new global norms around accountability, human rights, and transitional justice.

The wars that followed the breakup of Yugoslavia in the early 1990s, alongside the 1994 genocide in Rwanda, revealed the systematic use of sexual violence as a weapon of war. In response, the United Nations established ad hoc tribunals—the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) and the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR)—which set historic legal precedents. For the first time, rape was prosecuted as a crime against humanity and an act of genocide, not merely a by-product of conflict. These tribunals laid the groundwork for the establishment of the International Criminal Court (ICC) in 2002, signalling a new era of international accountability. Feminist activists, legal scholars, and survivors were central in pushing for the recognition of gender-based violence within international law, reshaping global understandings of justice, and challenging impunity for crimes against women in conflict zones.

Simultaneously, the fall of apartheid in South Africa in 1994 after decades of grassroots resistance and international solidarity marked a symbolic and practical victory against institutionalised racism. The new democratic government under Nelson Mandela prioritised reconciliation, truth, and social justice, inspiring global struggles against inequality and exclusion. These milestones shaped a more interconnected global human rights agenda and offered models for post-conflict transformation and inclusive governance.

1990
A TURNING-POINT MEETING IN RIO

In 1990, DAWN held a landmark inter-regional meeting in Rio de Janeiro, bringing together over 100 participants to assess progress since the Nairobi Conference and respond to shifting global debates on population, environment, and development. Amid rising conservative backlash, the meeting marked a turning point—DAWN committed not just to critique, but to build feminist alternatives in partnership with allies, setting the stage for deeper engagement in global UN processes.

1992-1996

1992
GLOBAL AGENDAS, WOMEN’S VOICES: THE UN CONFERENCES OF THE 1990S

This decade saw a series of influential UN world conferences—on the environment (Rio, 1992), human rights (Vienna, 1993), population (Cairo, 1994), social development (Copenhagen, 1995), women’s rights (Beijing, 1995), and habitat (Istanbul, 1996). These gatherings reaffirmed and expanded global commitments to gender equality, sustainable development, and human rights, while amplifying voices from the Global South and civil society.

At the 1992 Earth Summit in Rio, the “Women’s Tent” provided an autonomous space for transnational feminist strategising, laying the foundation for cross-regional alliances throughout the decade. The 1993 Vienna Conference led to the adoption of the UN Declaration on the Elimination of Violence Against Women and the creation of the first UN Special Rapporteur on Violence Against Women in 1994—crucial milestones in institutionalising women’s rights within international human rights law. The 1995 Fourth World Conference on Women in Beijing culminated in the Beijing Platform for Action, a comprehensive agenda that placed gender equality at the heart of development, peacebuilding, and democratic governance. Together, these UN conferences catalysed a new era of intersectional and rights-based policymaking.

1992
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
FEMINIST PERSPECTIVES ON ENVIROMENT AND DEVELOPMENT: DAWN MOBILISES AT RIO SUMMIT

During the Earth Summit, DAWN held a panel on Debt and Trade, drafted a Declaration by Women, and discussed the burden on women of the devastating and diverse consequences of Structural Adjustment Policies. We also launched a DAWN booklet: Environment and Development: Grass Roots Women’s Perspective by Rosina Wiltshire.

The publication shows that, even in its early work, DAWN had a visionary grasp of environmental justice, linking gender, poverty, and sustainability long before these became mainstream concerns. We challenged overconsumption, technocratic fixes like population control, and global inequities, calling instead for locally grounded, feminist solutions centred on justice and human rights and the transformation of economic structures—issues now central to contemporary environmental justice movements.

1994
POPULATION AND REPRODUCTIVE RIGHTS: FEMINIST PERSPECTIVES FROM THE SOUTH

DAWN played a pivotal role in Cairo, in shifting the global population debate toward a framework rooted in sexual and reproductive health and rights. In the lead-up to the conference, DAWN launched “Population and Reproductive Rights: Feminist Perspectives from the South”, authored by Sonia Corrêa and based on three years of research and consultations with over 150 women from more than 50 countries. The platform bridged local realities and global debates, offering a powerful Southern feminist contribution to the Cairo agenda. DAWN also helped initiate the Women’s Declaration on Population Policies and acted as a crucial bridge between women’s rights groups from the global South and North, helping to overcome deep divisions. While policy outcomes were partial, DAWN’s role in framing key concepts—like “gender” and “sexual and reproductive rights”—was vital to the paradigm shift that continues to influence global discourse today.

As global debates on population and development gained momentum, “Population And Environment: Rethinking The Debate” brought together representatives of DAWN and other social science organisations in a joint effort to rethink the conversation. Representing DAWN, Rosina Wiltshire took part in the initiative. Sonia Corrêa and Peggy Antrobus were parte of the conversations leading up to the book, while Gita Sen contributed a key chapter on women, poverty, and population. DAWN brought a groundbreaking feminist perspective that challenged dominant, depoliticised narratives. The book marked a critical shift by placing population issues within broader social, economic, and political contexts, emphasising the lived realities of women in the Global South and calling for justice-based approaches to environmental sustainability.

September, 1995
DAWN’S PIVOTAL ROLE AT THE HISTORICAL BEIJING CONFERENCE

Ahead of the Beijing Conference, DAWN launched ‘‘Markers on the Way: the DAWN Debates on Alternative Development.” Coordinated by Gita Sen and shaped through regional consultations across the global South, the text reflected a collective feminist effort to confront the tensions between economic growth, human development, and environmental sustainability. At a time when governments and institutions avoided these contradictions, DAWN pushed for deeper civil society engagement to hold globalisation and the State accountable to people and the planet.

1999-2000

1999-2000
GENDER, WAR, AND THE UN RESOLUTIONS: RESOLUTIONS 1265 AND 1325 RECOGNISED GENDERED VIOLENCE AND WOMEN’S ROLES IN PEACE.

In the late 1990s and early 2000s, the UN Security Council adopted key resolutions that recognised gendered violence and women’s roles in peace and security. While celebrated as breakthroughs, these policies exposed the limitations of institutional feminism, especially from the vantage point of Global South feminists advocating for structural justice.

UN Resolution 1265 (1999) marked the Council’s first explicit concern with civilian protection in armed conflict, acknowledging the gendered nature of war. Yet it failed to address the enduring colonial, racial, and economic structures that expose Global South women to disproportionate harm. Following decades of grassroots mobilisation, particularly by organisations such as the Women’s International League for Peace and Freedom (WILPF) and regional feminist networks, Resolution 1325 (2000) was adopted, recognising women not only as victims but as vital actors in peacebuilding and conflict resolution. Despite its emphasis on participation, protection, and prevention, its top-down implementation has often been shaped by donor priorities, marginalising local, intersectional feminist knowledge. These resolutions signal both progress and co-optation, highlighting the need for continued critique and mobilisation from the Global South to ensure feminist agendas are not merely symbolically recognised but meaningfully realised.

2000
A GROUNDBREAKING VIEW ON THE MARKETISATION OF GOVERNANCE, BY VIVIENE TAYLOR

DAWN’s work on the theme of Political Restructuring and Social Transformation (PRST) began in 1998 and assumed priority within the network in 1999-2000. The work entailed developing a Southern feminist global analysis of the changing character of the state system, social movements and multilateralisms in the context of an emerging global governance complex during the era of economic globalisation. The resulting book, titled ‘Marketisation of Governance: Critical Feminist Perspectives from the South’, is DAWN’s third global analysis by Viviene Taylor.

With the book, this video was launched at the World Social Summit + 5 in Geneva in 2000. They present a strong critique of global financial and trade liberalisation, a process that has resulted in the erosion of the state’s capacity to meet national social development needs.

2001
OPPOSING DAVOS, WE MOBILSED ON THE WORLD SOCIAL FORUM IN BRAZIL

The World Social Forum (WSF) was born in 2001 in Porto Alegre, in opposition to the Davos World Economic Forum. With echoes in local, national, regional and thematic forums, the WSF quickly became a space for articulation and learning, marked by horizontal, self-managed processes to exchange experiences on global militancy and activism. DAWN participated in the construction of this space from its start. We walked through the agendas, proposing new spaces for dialogue and questioning when patriarchal relations were being reproduced within the movement and within the organisation of the Forum. In the following years, DAWN continued to be a part of the forum, promoting debates and fostering connections.

2003
Bangalore, India
SOWING SEEDS OF SOUTHERN FEMINIST LEADERSHIP: DAWN TRAINING INSTITUTE (DTI)

DAWN selected 28 young feminists from the South for a three-week training programme in Bangalore, India, from 14 September to 3 October 2003. Its objective was to upgrade advocacy skills and analytical capabilities, and particularly an understanding of interlinkages and power relationships. Participants were trained by experienced feminist advocates to assist them in understanding the changing terrain of the struggle for gender justice.

The 2nd DAWN Training Institute, held in Montevideo, Uruguay, ran for 20 days, from November 6 to 26, 2005. A total of 26 women from 16 different countries participated in the training. DAWN aimed to share its global gender justice advocacy analysis and experience, explain the evolving landscape and intersecting agendas of gender and development, and foster a new generation of global feminist advocates to strengthen future campaigns.

2006
QUEER RIGHTS, GLOBAL PRINCIPLES

Developed by a coalition of human rights experts in Yogyakarta, Indonesia, the Principles articulated legal standards for protecting LGBTQ+ people from violence, discrimination, and exclusion.

While not a UN document, the Principles have become an influential advocacy tool used by activists and legal advocates—especially in the Global South—to challenge criminalisation laws, promote gender justice, and demand state accountability. Yet, despite their progressive language, implementation has varied widely. Many countries in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East continue to enforce punitive laws against LGBTQ+ individuals, often underpinned by colonial-era legal codes and rising religious fundamentalism. Feminist movements in the Global South have criticised the top-down nature of some international rights frameworks, calling instead for locally rooted approaches that address intersecting oppressions related to class, race, and gender nonconformity.

INTERLINKING POLICY, POLITICS AND WOMEN’S REPRODUCTIVE RIGHTS

Coordinated by Sonia Corrêa, this DAWN report explores health sector reform, maternal mortality, and abortion in 12 countries across the Global South. The study builds on DAWN’s deep involvement in the ICPD process and its follow-up reviews, combining global policy engagement with national-level analysis to highlight the factors that have enabled or constrained implementation in different contexts.

2008

2008
CRISIS AND CARE: FEMINIST RESPONSES TO ECONOMIC COLLAPSE

The 2008 global financial crisis deepened economic inequalities, hitting women in the Global South hardest. As governments implemented austerity measures shaped by international financial institutions, public sector cuts and job losses disproportionately affected women, especially those in informal or precarious work.
Feminist movements across Latin America, Africa, and Asia denounced neoliberal prescriptions, highlighting how economic “recovery” often came at the expense of women’s livelihoods, health, and unpaid care work. Activists demanded redistributive policies rooted in social protection, labor rights, and access to land and credit. These calls reframed economic justice as inseparable from gender justice, challenging global economic governance and exposing the gendered nature of systemic crises.

2010
Feminists Reclaiming Economy. Gender & Ecological justice: The GEEJ Series

In acknowledgement of the urgent need for more effective and interlinked regional feminist responses from the economic South, involving and in support of women advocates working in areas of gender and development, DAWN organiseda series of regional consultations and training institutes bewteen 2010 and 2014 on “Strengthening Policy Analysis and Advocacy on Gender, Economic and Ecological Justice” – the GEEJ series in short.

The GEEJ series began in the Pacific in September 2010, followed by Africa in November 2010, Latin America and the Caribbean in March 2011, the Mekong Region in April 2012, the Pacific in October 2012 and Latin America in August 2013.

In 2014, DAWN brought together Young Southern Women Activists from different regions for an Inter-regional GEEJ consultation in Manila, Philippines.

From tightened abortion laws in Latin America and Africa to the policing of LGBTQ+ identities in Asia and the Middle East, religious and political conservatism reshaped national policies and public discourse. Yet this period also ignited powerful feminist resistance.

2012
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
REAFIRMING OUR FIGHT FOR ENVIRONMENTAL JUSTICE AT RIO+20

Two decades after the Earth Summit, the Rio+20 Conference reaffirmed the importance of effectively engaging all sectors of civil society in UN processes related to sustainable development, including the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

From late 2011 to mid-2012, DAWN, in partnership with IWHC, RESURJ, YCSRR, and allies, actively participated in Rio+20 negotiations to push for the inclusion of women’s and young people’s sexual and reproductive health and rights within sustainable development. Despite setbacks in the final outcome document, the coalition helped secure references to ICPD and Beijing commitments and maintained a human rights framing in the Health and Gender sections. The process also strengthened cross-movement alliances, particularly with youth and environmental groups, signalling the growing importance of unified advocacy for gender justice in global forums.

2013
LATIN AMERICAN CONFERENCE ON POPULATION AND DEVELOPMENT

At the 2013 Regional Conference on Population and Development in Montevideo, DAWN and other organisations brought together 35 young feminist activists from across Latin America and the Caribbean to engage in advocacy around the Conference’s outcome document. Representing a wide range of movements—including sexual and reproductive rights, youth participation, Indigenous rights, and comprehensive sexuality education—the group aimed to ensure that equality, human rights, and the lived realities of marginalised communities were at the centre of the post-Cairo development agenda. Their participation marked a key moment in advancing intersectional and youth-led feminist advocacy in regional policy spaces.

2014
DAWN LAUNCHES A NEW GLOBAL RESEARCH ON SRHR

“Breaking Through the Development Silos: Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights, Millennium Development Goals and Gender Equity” is a case studies collection launched to provide useful evidence to support global advocacy on SRHR and gender equality in the context of the upcoming Cairo+20 and MDG+15 review processes in 2014 and 2015. Through this global research, DAWN demonstrate the phenomenon of “siloization” or the fragmentation and isolation of important social and human-rights issues from poverty agendas. This book provides useful evidence to support global advocacy on SRHR and gender equality in the context of the upcoming Cairo+20 and MDG+15 review processes in 2014 and 2015.

2015
BEYOND INCLUSION: FEMINIST CRITIQUES OF THE SDGS

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) marked a commitment to gender equality, with SDG 5 calling for an end to violence against women, equal economic participation, and access to reproductive rights. Although the SDGs explicitly named gender equality and intersectional development targets, they were rooted in neoliberal development frameworks, which sidestep the structural roots of inequality—colonial legacies, extractivist economies, and debt dependency.

2015
ASSESSING PROGRESS 20 YEARS AFTER CAIRO

At the 48th Session of the Commission on Population and Development (CPD) in April 2015, DAWN co-sponsored a UN side event alongside the governments of the Philippines and Denmark to launch a special issue of “Global Public Health”, the outcome of a two-year project coordinated by DAWN. The publication brought together key researchers and analysts to assess progress on SRHR in low- and middle-income countries and to reflect on how these issues should inform and be integrated into the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

2017

2017
LONG BEFORE #METOO: FEMINIST STRUGGLE AGAINST GENDER VIOLENCE

The global spotlight on #MeToo in 2017 intensified conversations around sexual violence, but women in the Global South had long been confronting these injustices. While #MeToo captured Western media attention, movements such as Egypt’s “We Are All Laila” (2006), India’s anti-rape protests (2012), Ni Una Menos (Not one [woman] less) in Argentina, and South Africa’s #RUReferenceList (2016) had already exposed the pervasiveness of sexual harassment and state indifference.

In 2017, #MeToo provided a discursive opening for survivors worldwide, but its framing often overlooked the decades-long struggles led by feminists in Africa, Asia, and Latin America who had been organizing in workplaces, campuses, and streets. In Nigeria, for example, #ArewaMeToo challenged both patriarchal violence and religious conservatism. In Argentina and across Latin America, Ni Una Menos—founded in 2015—had already mobilized mass protests against femicide and state impunity. These movements were deeply intersectional, connecting gender-based violence to structural racism, class inequalities, and postcolonial oppression. While #MeToo spurred policy debates globally, Global South feminists critiqued the movement’s initial focus on elite, urban, and often Western voices. Their work emphasized the need for contextual responses that address militarism, migration, economic precarity, and legal systems that frequently fail survivors. From Jakarta to Johannesburg, their resistance underscored that feminist struggles against sexual violence did not begin in 2017—nor were they confined to hashtags.

2018-2020
LATIN AMERICA’S WOMEN CHALLENGE DEEPLY ENTRENCHED CONSERVATISM

Latin America witnessed feminist mobilisation for reproductive rights. Led by Argentina’s marea verde (“green wave”), activists reframed abortion as a matter of justice, health, and human rights. The movement’s mass protests, legal advocacy, and public education campaigns sparked transnational solidarity across the region.
Although Argentina’s Voluntary Interruption of Pregnancy Bill was initially rejected in 2018, the campaign catalysed feminist action throughout the continent—from Mexico to Chile and Colombia.

2019

2019
THE NAIROBI SUMMIT – ICPD+25

To advance and chart progress of the ICPD Program of Action held in Cairo in 1994, and celebrate the 25th Anniversary, the Nairobi Summit that brought together governments, women’s groups and youth networks along with other UN agencies and the private sector.. Renewed donor commitments were made to the tune of USD 9 billion to ‘achieve zero maternal deaths, zero unmet needs for family planning, zero gender based violence and harmful practices’ by 2030. The summit ended on a high note as several stakeholders committed to advance and uphold the human rights and sexual and reproductive health and rights of women, adolescents and youth. The summit also pledged to intensify system-wide efforts to eliminate all preventable maternal and child mortality and morbidities, eradicate gender based violence, to support national governments in the full and accelerated implementation of the ICPD agenda, in line with implementation of national Sustainable Development Goals.

2019 – 2024
THE POLITICAL ECONOMY OF CONFLICT AND VIOLENCE AGAINST WOMEN

In 2009, DAWN began a process of exploring women’s activism and agency in times of war, conflict and transition through a series of self-reflective case studies written by feminist activists from Indonesia, Sri Lanka, Nepal and India.

In 2019, DAWN launched the book “The Political Economy of Conflict and Violence against Women” to show how political, economic, social and ideological processes intersect to shape conflict-related, gender-based violence against women, bringing together the work of a group of feminists from the global South.

DAWN also hosted two online and one in-person between 2022 and 2024, in collaboration with Masimanyane Women’s Rights International, with the purpose of collectively build knowledge and analysis on gender-based violence from a political economy lens, drawing from the everyday experiences of the participants who are involved in different capacities in responding to gender-based violence.

2020
PANDEMIC FAULT LINES: FEMINIST STRUGGLES AMID COVID-19

The COVID-19 pandemic laid bare and deepened pre-existing structural inequalities, hitting women and other structurally excluded communities—especially in the Global South—hardest with lockdowns, collapse of informal economies, domestic violence, unpaid care work, and caregiving responsibilities. Feminist organisations mobilised rapidly, offering mutual aid, legal and psychological support, and advocating for gender-responsive policy responses.

The pandemic also exposed the colonial hierarchies of the global health system. Wealthy states and pharmaceutical corporations resisted calls for the #TRIPSWaiver—a temporary suspension of intellectual property rights on vaccines and treatments—leaving low- and middle-income countries with limited access to life-saving technologies. Feminist and health justice movements denounced this as a form of medical apartheid, demanding the decolonisation of global health governance and equitable access to medicines.

At the same time, a renewed wave of resistance emerged against austerity measures that predated and were exacerbated by the pandemic. Campaigns like End Austerity drew attention to the gendered impact of decades of neoliberal structural adjustment policies—privatised health care, gutted public services, and debt regimes that stripped states of social protections. Feminist activists across Latin America, Africa, and Asia argued that pandemic recovery could not be separated from the broader fight against economic injustice and called for an end to austerity-driven governance. This period revealed the high cost of neoliberal health and economic systems, and reaffirmed the centrality of feminist analysis and organising in responding to global crises.

2020
FEMINIST FOR A PEOPLE’S VACCINE CAMPAIGN

Kicked off at the beginning of the pandemic, the Feminists for a People’s Vaccine (FPV) campaign, led by DAWN and Third World Network (TWN), advocates for equitable, affordable, and accessible COVID-19 vaccines, medicines, and technologies. Grounded in feminist principles of justice and solidarity, in the past five years the campaign has produced a rich body of work including a series of Issue Papers analysing global access to medicines and inequalities broadened by the pandemic from a Southern feminist perspective, covering topics like the TRIPS Waiver and corporate control of health technologies. Complementing this are podcasts, cartoons, and multilingual resources designed to make complex issues accessible to broader audiences. Through regional workshops, social mobilisation, and targeted advocacy, FPV has strengthened feminist networks and helped amplify Southern voices in global health debates. Together, these resources not only expose the injustices of the current global health system but also offer critical insights and strategies for shaping more just and feminist responses to future pandemics.

2020
BEYOND GREEN CAPITALISM: FEMINIST STRUGGLES FOR CLIMATE JUSTICE

Feminist climate justice campaigns – for many years before the 2020s) foreground structural inequality: colonial land theft, capitalist extractivism, and state-backed environmental violence. Across Latin America, Africa, and Asia, feminist groups demanded just transitions that centre care economies, land sovereignty, and the epistemologies of women as environmental stewards.

From La Via Campesina to Indigenous women defending the Amazon and anti-extractivist movements across the Andes and Sahel, these struggles link climate resilience with gender, racial, and intergenerational justice. In contrast to technocratic and market-based solutions promoted by Global North institutions, Global South feminist movements challenge the digital-industrial ‘green transition’ when it replicates extractive logics (e.g., lithium mining,deep sea mining, land grabs for solar farms, etc). They call instead for climate reparations, democratic governance of resources, and the decolonisation of environmental policy-making. By rooting climate justice in anti-patriarchal, anti-racist, and anti-capitalist frameworks, feminist actors reshape what a liveable and just future can look like—one where survival is not reduced to resilience, but built on justice, autonomy, and care.

June, 2020
TRYING TO MAKE SENSE OF AN UNPRECEDENT CRISES

As the pandemic took over the world, DAWN promoted DAWN Talks, a series of conversations with Southern feminists exploring how the crisis exposed and deepened existing inequalities. The discussions covered themes such as biopolitics, social protection for informal workers, intellectual property, HIV and necropolitics, and feminist reflections from China, highlighting both the depth of the crisis and the extraordinary state responses it provoked.

2020
FEMINISTS DEMAND FOR DIGITAL JUSTICE

The accelerated digital transformation raises feminist concerns around access, surveillance, labour, and data governance. Feminist movements have drawn attention to the digital divide, where poor and rural women face limited access to digital tools, infrastructure, and literacy. Simultaneously, the rise of platform-based labour has led to new forms of precarious work, often without protections.

Feminists critique the growing power of tech monopolies and data colonialism, calling for decolonial digital justice that safeguard privacy, resist surveillance, and promote equitable access. Feminist digital justice frameworks insist on the democratisation of digital technologies, recognition of gendered online violence, and support for feminist tech innovation, particularly by and for communities in the Global South.

2020
FEMINIST RESISTANCE TO GREEN EXTRACTIVISM

Deep-sea mining threatens marine ecosystems and jeopardizes the livelihoods of coastal and island communities already on the frontlines of climate change. In the Pacific, where exploratory licenses are most concentrated, women-led coalitions have mobilised against mining plans that ignore Indigenous sovereignty, environmental justice, and gendered impacts on food security and health.

Much like terrestrial mining, these oceanic ventures are marked by asymmetrical power—dominated by Global North corporations and shielded by weak international governance through bodies like the International Seabed Authority. Feminist critiques draw attention to how “green” development narratives can obscure the violence of extraction and environmental degradation, reinforcing patriarchal and racialised hierarchies.

In partnership with the Pacific Network on Globalisation (PANG), the Blue Economy project examines ocean-based development from a Southern feminist and deeply Pacific perspective. It tracks regional and global trends, including corporate-state initiatives such as deep-sea mining (DSM), in the context of the escalating climate crisis, raising urgent questions about who benefits from this agenda. ADVOCATING FOR GENDER JUSTICE AND ACCOUNTABILITY

DAWN has a history of engaging with Treaty Bodies, particularly with the Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (CESCR) and the CEDAW Committee, which monitors the implementation of the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW). We take part in these processes with the submission of shadow reports, training of civil society organisations to prepare shadow reports, delivering statements, and other forms of advocacy aimed at holding States accountable for the implementation of their human rights obligations and extraterritorial obligations (ETOs) based on existing international law. Our reports have addressed nuclear testing in French Polynesia, Australia’s actions abroad, Japan’s ocean dumping of radioactive wastewater, and Canadian involvement in deep-sea mining, highlighting their gendered impacts in the Pacific.

Corporations across the globe are capturing more and more of the public sphere, encroaching on all aspects of people’s lives. 2024
ICPD+30: RECLAIMING RIGHTS AMID GLOBAL ROLLBACK.

ICPD+30 is not just an anniversary but a battleground for resisting regression and demanding justice-centred, decolonial approaches to population policies. The 1994 ICPD marked a paradigm shift away from population control towards a framework grounded in sexual and reproductive health and rights (SRHR), gender equality, and development.

Nonetheless, reproductive rights are being rolled back across continents, authoritarian regimes are emboldened by anti-gender ideologies, and structural inequalities—worsened by climate breakdown, debt burdens, and pandemics—continue to restrict access to essential services. From Sub-Saharan Africa to Latin America and Southeast Asia, feminist advocates have used ICPD+30 as a platform to highlight unfinished business and emerging challenges: the criminalisation of abortion, corporate capture of health systems, unsafe migration, digital exclusion, and the securitisation of population policies. Movements demand that governments move beyond rhetoric, ensuring that SRHR is fully funded, intersectional, and responsive to the lived realities of marginalized communities. ICPD+30 reopens debates on the meaning of “development” itself, with feminists calling for justice-oriented frameworks that centre care, autonomy, and collective wellbeing over demographic targets or market-driven health agendas.

2025
BEIJING+30: RECKONING WITH FRAGILITY, REBUILDING WITH FEMINIST POWER

The 1995 Beijing Declaration was a historic commitment to advancing women’s rights across 12 critical areas—from education and health to political participation and violence prevention. However, as Beijing+30 arrives in 2025, feminist movements—particularly in the Global South—highlight how structural inequalities have been exacerbated by intersecting crises.
Feminist actors across Africa, Latin America, Asia, and the Middle East are reclaiming Beijing+30 not as a commemoration, but as a mobilisation: to reclaim decision-making power, dismantle neo-colonial global governance structures, and build alliances across movements—from climate justice and anti-racism to labour and Indigenous sovereignty. Rather than mere inclusion in broken systems, they demand transformation. Rooted in decolonial, intersectional, and anti-capitalist frameworks, Beijing+30 revitalises a feminist vision for justice that is systemic, not symbolic.

What does it mean to be 40?

What does it mean to be 40? That’s the question we’ve been asking ourselves. DAWN turns 40 this year, marking four decades of feminist research, activism, and solidarity across the global South. To celebrate, we launched our first Fellowship for young feminists from the global South. The anniversary will also be marked by new books and fresh projects that carry our legacy forward. This interactive timeline is part of that celebration—an invitation to reflect on the rise of a modern Southern feminist movement, as seen through the key moments, interventions, and struggles that have shaped DAWN’s journey since 1984.

As we look to the future, we carry forward a spirit of resistance rooted in the local and connected to the global. Our analysis begins in lived realities and moves outward to confront systemic injustice. As DAWN has always insisted, we were told development was enough, but we demanded more. We saw the cracks in the system and called for transformation, not reform. We have won victories, but backlash rises, and the struggle continues. In the next period, that’s where we’re going—toward deeper resistance, sharper analysis, and collective action grounded in feminist visions for another world.

Correct organizing axes (replacing false style/FX binary)
Axis 1 — Domain
Audio vs. Visual. Clean, non-overlapping. The only axis from the previous map that holds.
Axis 2 — Vocabulary type
Aesthetic (what it looks/sounds like) vs. Operational (who does it, at which pipeline stage). Most terms straddle both — that is the real source of confusion.
Axis 3 — Diegetic status
Diegetic: exists within the fiction (a lamp in the scene). Non-diegetic: imposed by the author (a stylistic flare, a score). Several terms live on both sides depending on use.
Axis 4 — Pipeline stage
Pre-production (design/concept), Production (capture/record), Post-production (DI, VFX, sound mix). Terms are not interchangeable across stages.

Term definitions — corrected (click to expand)
Soundtrack
audiodiegetic or non-diegetic
Composed or licensed music synchronized to the work. Can be non-diegetic (score — exists only for the audience) or diegetic (source music — playing within the scene). The distinction matters: diegetic music can be interrupted, reacted to, or turned off by characters. Owned by composer and music supervisor. Delivered as stems to the re-recording mixer.
Overlap zone: Sound FX — when compositional sound design is used as score (e.g. Zimmer's Interstellar, industrial music). The border dissolves intentionally in these cases.
Sound FX
audiodiegetic or non-diegetic
Designed audio events attached to actions, objects, or transitions. Diegetic when tied to on-screen events (footsteps, gunshots, doors). Non-diegetic when used editorially (whooshes on title cards, stylized impact frames). Produced by sound designers and Foley artists in post; some captured on set by the production sound department.
Overlap zone: Soundtrack — in ambient, industrial, and scored-effects work. Also overlaps with graphic FX when sound accompanies visual transitions (a motion graphic whoosh is both a graphic FX and a sound FX).
Visual FX (VFX) — corrected scope
visualpostdiegetic or non-diegetic
In the production industry, VFX means: compositing, CGI, matte painting, wire removal, environment replacement — i.e. content modification or augmentation of live-action footage. It does NOT contain color grading (DI) or motion graphics, which are separate disciplines with separate credits, tools, and vendors.
Previous map error: "Visual FX ⊃ {light FX, color FX, graphic FX}" was incorrect. VFX may receive a light FX pass (e.g. a CG lens flare) and may composite motion graphics, but those are collaboration points, not containment. Color grading is DI, owned by the colorist, not the VFX supervisor.
Graphic FX (motion graphics)
visualpostnon-diegetic (typically)
Title cards, lower thirds, stylized transitions, kinetic typography, animated overlays. Produced by motion graphics artists in post using tools like After Effects or Cinema 4D. Typically non-diegetic (the audience sees them; characters do not). Composited into the final picture but produced independently of the VFX pipeline.
Operational overlap with VFX: graphic FX elements are often delivered to the VFX department for compositing into a shot. That is a handoff, not containment.
Light style
visualproductiondiegetic or non-diegetic
The DP's recurring aesthetic language for lighting: hardness/softness, directionality, color temperature, motivated vs. unmotivated sources. Decided in pre-production (lookbook, references) and executed on set. Diegetic when light sources exist in the scene (a window, a candle); non-diegetic when light is unmotivated and purely stylistic.
Physical co-determination with color style: the color temperature of light sources on set directly determines the color palette. A DP choosing tungsten vs. daylight makes a color style decision simultaneously. These are not separate — they are one physical decision expressed in two vocabularies.
Light FX
visualproduction or postdiegetic or non-diegetic
Specific lighting effects: lens flares, god rays, volumetric shafts, light leaks, halos. Can be practical (achieved on set by the DP with physical gels, practical sources, or lens choices) or synthetic (added in VFX or DI in post). Same aesthetic result; different production route and different crew credit.
This is one of the few terms that genuinely spans production and post — the same effect can be created in either stage. It is therefore an aesthetic term (what it looks like) more than an operational one.
Color style
visualpre + postnon-diegetic
The color language of the work: palette, saturation, hue bias, contrast relationship. Decided conceptually in pre-production (lookbook, reference stills) and executed in post by the colorist in the DI. Always non-diegetic — characters do not perceive the grade. Owned aesthetically by the director and DP; executed operationally by the colorist.
Near-synonym with color FX in practice: the DI session where color style is defined is the same session where color FX passes are applied. The distinction is conceptual (intent vs. technique), not operational.
Color FX
visualpost — DInon-diegetic
Specific color transformations applied as effects: LUTs, bleach bypass, cross-processing simulation, sky replacement grades, selective color isolation. Applied by the colorist in DI using tools like DaVinci Resolve. Operationally identical to color style — both happen in the same DI session — but color FX refers to specific, identifiable passes rather than the overall aesthetic direction.
DI is not VFX. The colorist is not a VFX artist. The tools, deliverables, union classifications, and vendor relationships are separate. Color FX lives in the DI pipeline, not the VFX pipeline.
Graphics style
visualpre-productionnon-diegetic
The overall visual design language: line quality, rendering approach, typographic system, spatial grammar, palette logic. Set in pre-production. In film: the production design and title sequence aesthetic. In illustration/comics: the mark-making system (line weight, fill, halftone, hatching, panel structure). Not a synonym for "graphic novel" — it is the language; graphic novel is one of many formats that language can appear in.
Graphic novel — corrected: format, not style
formatpre-production
A publication format: long-form sequential art in book form. Not a style. The visual styles within graphic novels span ligne claire (Hergé, Moebius), high-contrast ink (Frank Miller), painted realism (Alex Ross), collage (Dave McKean), and many others. Invoking "graphic novel" as a visual reference is invoking a format that contains dozens of incompatible styles. The useful question is: which specific graphic novel visual conventions — panel borders, speech bubble morphology, halftone, ink weight, color flatness — are being referenced?
Previous map error: "graphic novel ⊂ graphics style" was a category inversion. A graphic novel is a container for a graphics style, not a subtype of one.
Corrected overlaps — three types
Light style ↔ color style — physical co-determination
Color temperature of light sources on set determines the color palette. One physical decision; two vocabulary entries. Real and unavoidable overlap.
Color style ≈ color FX — same DI session
Both executed by the colorist in DI. The distinction is conceptual (aesthetic intent vs. applied pass), not operational. Near-synonym in practice.
Soundtrack ∩ sound FX — when sound design is compositional
In works like Interstellar, The Social Network, or Apocalypse Now's helicopter opening, the border dissolves intentionally. Sound FX become the score.
Light FX — production OR post (same aesthetic, different crew)
A lens flare can be practical (DP on set) or CG (VFX in post). Same visual result; different pipeline, credit, and cost. Operational split, not semantic.
Graphic FX ↔ VFX — handoff, not containment
Motion graphics are often delivered to VFX for compositing into shots. That is a pipeline handoff between two separate disciplines, not one containing the other.
VFX does NOT contain color grading — previous map error
Color grading is DI. Separate tools (DaVinci Resolve), separate credit (colorist), separate vendor, separate union. The colorist does not report to the VFX supervisor.
